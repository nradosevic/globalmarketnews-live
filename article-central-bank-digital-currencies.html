Central Bank Digital Currencies: The Race to Reshape Money

By: Thomas Bergström Published: February 25, 2026 Category: Technology

The concept of money is undergoing its most fundamental transformation since the abandonment of the gold standard. Central bank digital currencies—digital forms of sovereign money issued directly by central banks—have moved from theoretical curiosity to active development in over 100 countries. China's digital yuan is already operational in multiple cities; the European Central Bank has committed to launching a digital euro by 2028; and even the Federal Reserve, initially skeptical, has intensified research into a potential digital dollar. The implications for banking, payments, and monetary policy are profound.

Understanding CBDCs requires distinguishing them from existing digital money. When consumers pay with a debit card or send a Venmo payment, they're moving commercial bank money—deposits that represent claims on private banks, not the central bank itself. CBDCs would be direct liabilities of the central bank, equivalent in status to physical cash but existing entirely in digital form. This distinction matters enormously for questions of counterparty risk, privacy, and the structure of the financial system.

Motivations for CBDC development vary by country. In economies where cash usage remains high, like Sweden and Japan, CBDCs offer a way to modernize payment systems while maintaining central bank money as a payment option. In emerging markets with large unbanked populations, CBDCs could expand financial inclusion by providing digital money to citizens without bank accounts. For China, the digital yuan serves both domestic policy goals and ambitions to internationalize the renminbi and reduce dependence on dollar-denominated payment systems.

The design choices facing CBDC developers are consequential and contentious. Should individuals hold accounts directly at the central bank, or should CBDCs be distributed through existing banks and payment providers? Direct accounts would disintermediate commercial banks and could destabilize the financial system during periods of stress, as depositors might flee private banks for the safety of central bank money. Intermediated models preserve the banking system's role but add complexity and limit some potential benefits.

Privacy represents perhaps the most sensitive design dimension. Cash transactions are anonymous; most electronic payments are not. CBDCs could theoretically enable unprecedented surveillance of financial activity—a feature that some governments might view as a benefit for tax enforcement and anti-money-laundering, but that raises profound civil liberties concerns. Technical approaches like zero-knowledge proofs could enable privacy-preserving CBDCs, but the political will to implement such protections varies considerably.

For the banking industry, CBDCs represent both threat and opportunity. If citizens can hold digital money directly at the central bank, will they still need bank deposits? Banks could see their funding base erode and their role in the payment system diminished. Conversely, banks that position themselves as CBDC infrastructure providers could capture new revenue streams. The outcome depends heavily on design choices that remain unresolved in most jurisdictions.

The geopolitical dimension of CBDC competition deserves attention. China's head start in CBDC development—the digital yuan has processed billions of dollars in transactions—gives it a potential advantage in shaping global standards and practices. If the digital yuan becomes widely used in international trade, it could gradually erode dollar hegemony in ways that have strategic implications for the United States. The race to develop CBDCs is not merely a technical exercise; it's a contest to define the future architecture of global finance.
